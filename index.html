üìê How the Recession Risk Score Is Calculated

The Recession Risk Score is a composite indicator built from eight key U.S. economic series (Treasury yields, unemployment, jobless claims, GDP, industrial production, consumer sentiment, credit spreads, and housing starts). The formula is transparent so anyone can reproduce it directly from FRED data.

1. Start from a baseline

We begin with a neutral baseline risk of 15%. This represents a normal, healthy-growth environment with no strong recession signals.

risk = 15

2. Adjust using key indicators

Each indicator nudges the score up or down depending on whether it looks supportive or stressful for the economy.

  • Yield curve: inversions add risk; steep positive curves reduce it.
  • Labor market: rising unemployment and jobless claims add risk.
  • Growth: weak or negative real GDP adds risk.
  • Sentiment: pessimistic consumers add risk.
  • Credit spreads: wider BBB spreads add risk.

3. Clamp to a realistic range

Finally, the score is bounded between 5% and 95% to avoid implying either zero probability or absolute certainty.

risk = max(5, min(95, risk))
